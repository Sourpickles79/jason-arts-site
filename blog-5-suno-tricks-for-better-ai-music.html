5 Suno Tricks for Better AI Music

Suno is easy to get decent results out of and genuinely hard to get great results out of. The gap between the two is mostly prompting habits, not luck. These are five things that consistently move the needle in my own sessions for Jason Arts RecordsJason Arts Records.

1. Use structure tags, not just a vibe description

Writing "upbeat pop song about summer" gets you something generic. Bracketed structure tags like [Verse], [Chorus], [Bridge], and [Instrumental Break] give the model an actual song shape to build around instead of guessing where sections should land. Songs with tagged structure come back sounding like songs, not loops.

2. Get specific about instrumentation, not just genre

"Lo-fi hip hop" is a genre. "Lo-fi hip hop, dusty upright piano, vinyl crackle, soft brushed drums, no bass until the second verse" is a production brief. The more concrete the instrumentation detail, the less the model falls back on generic genre defaults.

3. Reference a mood arc, not just a mood

Instead of "melancholy song," try describing where it starts and where it ends: "starts sparse and hesitant, builds to a full wall of sound by the final chorus." Suno responds well to arc language because it maps naturally onto verse/chorus dynamics.

4. Lock in a persona for consistency across a project

If you're building more than one track that's supposed to feel like the same "artist," reuse the same vocal style and tone descriptors word-for-word across prompts. Small wording drift between sessions is one of the most common reasons a two-track project ends up sounding like two different bands.

5. Don't skip the regenerate pass

The first generation is a draft, not a final. Running the same prompt two or three times and picking the strongest take is standard practice, not a fallback - even with an identical prompt, Suno's variation between takes is often bigger than the difference between two different prompts.

None of these tricks are complicated. They're just the difference between describing a genre and directing a session.
